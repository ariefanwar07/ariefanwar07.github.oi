How Have Indonesia's Trade Dynamics Evolved Over the Past Three Decades?

This project investigates how Indonesia's trade dynamics have evolved over the past three decades and identifies the key factors influencing its trade balance.

  • Aims:
    • Analyze Indonesia's trade performance and its driving factors over the past three decades.
    • Identify key trade partners, commodities, and external factors influencing Indonesia’s trade balance.
    • Evaluate the effectiveness of economic policies and agreements like IJEPA.
  • Data:
    • Sources:
      • World Bank data on exports, imports, trade balance, and commodity prices.
      • UN Comtrade data on key commodities, trade partners, and bilateral trade agreements.
    • How Data Was Accessed:
      • World Bank: Data was retrieved programmatically using the World Bank API. Python scripts written in Google Colab leveraged the requests library for API queries and pandas for data processing.
      • UN Comtrade: Data was accessed via their publicly available web interface, with queries filtered to focus on key commodities and trade partners. Data was manually downloaded for analysis. While UN Comtrade offers an API for more advanced data retrieval, full access requires a subscription. For instance, the Premium Individual plan costs $2,000 per year and includes individual access, up to 5,000 queries per day, and internal-use terms.
    • Automation and Replication:
      • Python scripts automated data retrieval from the World Bank API, ensuring consistent data collection and processing.
      • For UN Comtrade data, manual downloads were supplemented with custom Python scripts to process and standardize the datasets for analysis.
      • All workflows and scripts have been documented in the project's Google Colab Notebooks, enabling replication with minimal manual effort.

    • Analytical Challenges

    • Data Cleaning and Analysis:
      • Standardizing trade data into a consistent tabular format suitable for analysis was challenging due to inconsistent formats and missing values across datasets. This was resolved using Python libraries like `pandas` for data wrangling and cleaning.
      • Integrating diverse datasets, such as country codes (ISO3) and HS codes for commodities, required cross-referencing multiple sources. Tools like `pandas` and `merge` functions were used to ensure accurate alignment.
    • Data Visualization:
      • Balancing detail and clarity in visualizations, particularly for complex datasets, was addressed using Vega-Lite for interactive and flexible chart creation.
      • Ensuring compatibility across platforms was achieved by embedding visualizations directly into web pages using Vega-Lite JSON specifications and the `vega-embed` library.
      • Addressing challenges in visualizing multi-dimensional data involved breaking down datasets into simpler subsets and using interactive charts to allow users to explore specific dimensions.

    Conclusions

    • Indonesia's trade balance has improved significantly since 2020, driven by increased exports and economic recovery post-COVID-19.
    • Key export commodities like palm oil and coal dominate revenue, while imports emphasize industrial reliance on foreign technology.
    • China, Japan, and ASEAN countries are vital trade partners, underscoring Indonesia's regional and global integration.
    • Singapore remains Indonesia's dominant trade partner in ASEAN, leveraging its strategic location and advanced infrastructure, while Viet Nam is rapidly emerging as a key partner, driven by industrial growth and integration into global supply chains.
    • Price fluctuations in commodities like coal and palm oil significantly influence export revenues, highlighting Indonesia’s vulnerability to global markets.
    • The IJEPA agreement has positively impacted Indonesia's trade to Japan.
    • Economic diversification remains a key challenge, as Indonesia's heavy reliance on natural resources exposes the economy to global market volatility.
    • Despite improvements in trade, there is a growing need for value-added manufacturing to reduce dependency on raw commodity exports.
    • Indonesia's focus on renewable energy and sustainable practices offers potential opportunities to expand trade in green technologies and services.
    • Future economic policies should focus on strengthening domestic industries and improving trade agreements to ensure long-term growth and stability.
