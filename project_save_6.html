How Have Indonesia's Trade Dynamics Evolved Over the Past Three Decades?

This project investigates how Indonesia's trade dynamics have evolved over the past three decades and identifies the key factors influencing its trade balance.

Data Sources: World Bank API, UN Comtrade

  • Aims:
    • To analyze Indonesia's trade performance and its driving factors over the past three decades.
    • To identify key trade partners, commodities, and external factors influencing Indonesia’s trade balance.
    • To evaluate the effectiveness of economic policies and agreements like IJEPA.
  • Data:
    • World Bank data on exports, imports, trade balance, and commodity prices.
    • UN Comtrade data on key commodities, trade partners, and bilateral trade agreements.

Analytical Challenges

  • Data Collection:
    • Inconsistent reporting formats across data sources (e.g., World Bank vs. UN Comtrade).
    • Difficulty in accessing up-to-date trade data due to time lags in reporting.
  • Data Transformation:
    • Standardizing trade data into a consistent tabular format suitable for analysis.
    • Integrating diverse datasets, such as country codes (ISO3) and HS codes for commodities.
    • Dealing with outliers and anomalous values in historical trade figures.
  • Data Visualization:
    • Balancing detail and clarity in visualizations, particularly for complex datasets.
    • Ensuring compatibility across platforms.
    • Addressing challenges in visualizing multi-dimensional data.

Conclusions

  • Indonesia's trade balance has improved significantly since 2020, driven by increased exports and economic recovery post-COVID-19.
  • Key export commodities like palm oil and coal dominate revenue, while imports emphasize industrial reliance on foreign technology.
  • China, Japan, and ASEAN countries are vital trade partners, underscoring Indonesia's regional and global integration.
  • Singapore remains Indonesia's dominant trade partner in ASEAN, leveraging its strategic location and advanced infrastructure, while Viet Nam is rapidly emerging as a key partner, driven by industrial growth and integration into global supply chains.
  • Price fluctuations in commodities like coal and palm oil significantly influence export revenues, highlighting Indonesia’s vulnerability to global markets.
  • The IJEPA agreement has positively impacted Indonesia's trade to Japan.
  • Economic diversification remains a key challenge, as Indonesia's heavy reliance on natural resources exposes the economy to global market volatility.
  • Despite improvements in trade, there is a growing need for value-added manufacturing to reduce dependency on raw commodity exports.
  • Indonesia's focus on renewable energy and sustainable practices offers potential opportunities to expand trade in green technologies and services.
  • Future economic policies should focus on strengthening domestic industries and improving trade agreements to ensure long-term growth and stability.
